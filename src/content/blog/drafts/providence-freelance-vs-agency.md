I should say upfront: I'm a freelancer. I have an obvious stake in how this comparison comes out. I'm going to try to be fair anyway, because the honest answer is that the right choice genuinely depends on your project.

## The local freelancer

Direct access to one person. You know who's working on your project, you can call or text them, and they're not routing your questions through an account manager. For a lot of small business projects, that's exactly what you want.

Cost is usually lower than agencies — you're not paying for overhead, office space, or layers of management. A freelancer also tends to be more flexible about scope and timeline adjustments mid-project.

The real risk: there's no bench. If your freelancer gets sick, takes on too much at once, or goes on vacation, you feel it. There's no one to hand things off to. For businesses that need constant availability or have genuinely time-critical work, that's a meaningful limitation.

Freelancers work best for focused projects with a clear scope, ongoing relationships where communication and responsiveness matter, and businesses that want to build a working relationship with one person over time rather than managing a vendor relationship.

## Providence area agencies

A bigger team means more capacity for larger or more complex projects. If you need design, development, and strategy all at once, an agency can provide all three without you having to coordinate between separate people. Project management is usually built in.

Cost is higher — significantly higher, in some cases. You're paying for that infrastructure. Agencies also tend to move more deliberately: more process, more meetings, longer timelines. That's appropriate for large projects and can be frustrating for small ones.

The quality variance is real. Some Providence agencies are genuinely excellent. Others are essentially a few freelancers with an office and a higher rate. Ask to talk to past clients and look at actual work they've done, not just case studies on their website.

Agencies make the most sense for larger or more complex projects, teams that want a formal vendor relationship with contracts and SLAs, and situations where you need multiple disciplines at once.

## Remote and offshore teams

The appeal is cost — significantly lower rates, especially for offshore teams. If the spec is extremely clear and you can manage asynchronous communication, remote teams can deliver real value.

The risks are also real. Timezone gaps mean a question you ask at 9am might not get answered until the next morning. Communication overhead adds up. Quality is variable and harder to verify before you're already into the project. And you lose local knowledge and the kind of accountability that comes from someone who lives and works in the same community you do.

Remote teams work best when you have someone technical in-house to manage the relationship and catch issues, when the project is well-defined enough that communication can be minimal, and when cost is the primary constraint.

## How to decide

Think about the size of the project, how much management bandwidth you have, and how important local presence and accessibility are to you.

A $2,000 automation project is a bad fit for an agency — you'll spend more on kickoff meetings than the work costs. A $60,000 platform rebuild might be a bad fit for a solo freelancer, depending on the scope and timeline. A remote team for an ongoing project with no one in-house to manage it is a recipe for frustration.

The honest answer is there's no universally right choice. Talk to a couple of options, ask to see relevant work they've done, ask for a reference you can actually call, and go with whoever communicates clearly and seems to understand your problem.
