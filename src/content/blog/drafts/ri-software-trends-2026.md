Technology moves fast. Most of it isn't relevant to a 15-person service business in Cranston. The trick is figuring out which developments are actually useful versus which ones are just noise from people trying to sell you something.

Here's what I'm actually seeing make a difference for local Rhode Island businesses right now.

## AI for the boring stuff

There's been enough hype about artificial intelligence to last a decade. Set that aside.

What's actually happening at the small business level is quieter and more useful. Business owners are using AI tools to draft customer emails, summarize long documents, answer common inquiries outside of business hours, and knock out admin tasks that used to eat an hour a day.

None of this is transformative revolution. It's just genuinely useful time savings, and the barrier to entry is low. Most of these tools cost $20-30 a month and don't require any technical setup. If you haven't tried using something like ChatGPT or Claude for drafting business communications or working through a problem, it's worth an afternoon.

## Automation tools closing the gap

A few years ago, connecting two different software systems — say, automatically creating a customer record when someone fills out a contact form — required either custom development or someone fairly technical to set it up. That gap has narrowed considerably.

Tools like Make (formerly Integromat) and similar platforms have gotten genuinely capable. Simple automations that used to take days of development work can now be configured without code in a few hours. For small businesses, this means some problems have cheaper solutions than they used to. Not all problems — complex, custom workflows still often need real development work — but a surprising number of common ones.

## Mobile-first is the baseline now

This isn't a new trend, but the gap keeps widening between businesses that take mobile seriously and those that don't.

Local searches happen on phones. Someone looking for a contractor, a restaurant, a service business in the Providence area is almost certainly doing that search on their phone. If your site is hard to use on mobile — small text, buttons too close together, forms that are annoying to fill out on a touchscreen — you're losing people before they ever contact you.

If you haven't looked at your own site on your phone recently, do that today.

## What to ignore

Blockchain and Web3 solutions aren't relevant for the vast majority of local businesses. If someone is pitching you on this, ask them to explain specifically what problem it solves for your business. Usually there isn't a satisfying answer.

Anything that requires a six-month implementation timeline and an enterprise-level contract is probably not the right tool for a small business. There are usually simpler alternatives.

Bleeding-edge AI integrations that require significant technical infrastructure are worth watching but not worth rushing into. The tools are still evolving fast and the costs of early adoption are high.

## The practical filter

For any trend or tool you hear about, ask one question: does this solve a problem my business actually has?

If the answer is yes, it's worth a closer look. If the answer requires a lot of hedging — "well, it could potentially help if we changed how we handle X..." — it's probably noise.
