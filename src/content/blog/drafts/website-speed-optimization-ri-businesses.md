There's no shortage of advice about website speed. Most of it is technically accurate and practically irrelevant for a small local business. Here's what actually matters.

## The milliseconds myth

You'll hear that every millisecond counts. In some contexts — high-traffic e-commerce, financial platforms — that's true. For a local plumber, law firm, or restaurant, the difference between a 1.2-second and 1.8-second load time is not why you're losing business.

Below roughly 3 seconds on mobile, most users don't consciously notice a difference. The threshold that actually costs you customers is when the site *feels* sluggish — when things are visibly loading, buttons don't respond, or the page jumps around as content loads in. That's a real problem. Shaving 600 milliseconds off an already-acceptable site is not.

## The PageSpeed score trap

Google's PageSpeed Insights is a useful tool that gets treated like a report card. A 72 score on a site that's clean, mobile-friendly, and has solid local content will outrank a 95-score site with thin copy and no relevance to your area. The score is a proxy for good practices — not the goal itself.

Chasing a perfect score often means spending real money on diminishing returns. That time and budget are almost always better spent elsewhere.

## Not all fixes are worth doing

Website performance follows something like an 80/20 rule. A handful of things cause most of the problems. Fix those and you've done most of the work. The rest — advanced caching configurations, server-side rendering tweaks, micro-optimizations to render-blocking resources — can be worth addressing eventually, but they're not where a small business should start.

## What actually moves the needle

**Images.** Unoptimized images are the single most common speed problem on small business websites. An 8MB photo that could be a 200KB image without any visible quality difference is doing real damage. This is also the easiest fix — compress images before uploading, size them to the dimensions they'll actually be displayed at, and use modern formats where your platform supports it.

**Hosting.** The cheapest shared hosting has a real ceiling. If your site is on a crowded server, there's a limit to how fast it can be regardless of how well-optimized the code is. A decent mid-tier host makes a noticeable difference and usually costs less than $20/month.

**Bloat.** Page builders with dozens of bundled scripts, plugin stacks where half the plugins haven't been updated in two years, themes loaded with features you're not using — all of this adds up. Simpler is faster.

**Mobile performance specifically.** Local searches happen overwhelmingly on phones. A site that feels fine on your office WiFi on a desktop can be genuinely painful on mobile. Mobile performance is the number that matters most for a local business.

## The honest benchmark

Load your site on your phone on LTE, not office WiFi. Walk through it the way a new customer would. Does it feel usable? Do images load promptly? Does the contact form work without frustration?

That experience is closer to what your customers actually encounter than any score in a performance tool. Fix the things that make that experience bad, and you've done the work that matters.
